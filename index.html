Pedro Cardoso-Leite

Hi, I'm Pedro, a cognitive science researcher.


Find Out More

As a cognitive scientist, I work on how to use digital technologies to enhance assessment and learning. My ultimate goal is to design scientifically validated interventions that have a significant positive impact on people’s lives; in particular I’m interested by non-verbal games for kids which—contrary to traditional tests—are fair to those having language difficulties or test-anxiety.

To reach that goal I’ve developed over my career the diverse set of skills that are required for such an enterprise. During my PhD and first postdoc, I’ve extensively used psychophysical methods, eye tracking and brain imaging to better understand human cognitive skills, decision-making and learning at the single subject level. Over the last years at the University of Geneva I’ve learned with game designers to design engaging gamified experiments, to set-up large-scale online studies and to use machine learning and computational modeling to make sense of this type of data.

I’ve used these skills in a series of online experiments to assess interindividual difference when learning hierarchically structured information. In addition, we assess a variety of variables—cognitive, motivation, demographic, life-style and technology use—that determine why some people may learn faster than others and why some people are able to better transfer their knowledge. In parallel to this work, we’ve developed computational models to precisely account for participants learning abilities on the trial-by-trial level (as opposed to the classic psychometric approach that consists in summarizing performance in a global generic score like working memory capacity). We believe that this combination of scales is a powerful approach to further our understanding of human abilities to learn and transfer. Furthermore, the use of game-like tasks that rely minimally on language makes it easy to deploy these games in the general population. This has been confirmed by a recent pilot study we ran with primary school children in Switzerland.

Much work remains to be done. The design and production of intervention games requires a multidisciplinary team that understands both the principles underlying good game design, the mechanisms underlying brain plasticity and learning and how to harness these processes so that they can be useful in schools and in real-life settings. The scientific validation of these games furthermore requires a systematic access to schools and longitudinal data collection to assess effectiveness over the long-term.
